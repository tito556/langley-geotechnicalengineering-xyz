In Langley we often see projects stalled because boreholes alone can't resolve what sits between them—particularly in the glacial till and outwash deposits that cover much of the Township and City. Seismic tomography gives us that continuous image, stitching together velocity gradients across a line so you can pick out buried channels, weathered bedrock surfaces, and lateral changes that affect foundation design. Most of our work here runs through the Fraser Valley lowlands where water-saturated silts and sands create velocity contrasts that tomography handles well. We combine the geophysical data with our lab testing to ground-truth the interpretation—because a velocity model without material confirmation is just a pretty picture. For deeper investigations where bedrock dips below 30 m, seismic refraction provides the first-arrival control, while MASW adds shear-wave velocity profiles for site class determination under NBCC 2020.

A seismic tomogram between boreholes can reveal a buried channel or fractured rock zone that isolated logs completely miss.

Method and coverage

The Fraser Valley’s high water table—in Langley it commonly sits within 2 to 5 m of the surface—significantly affects seismic velocity measurements. Saturated silts can look deceptively fast on a P-wave tomogram, mimicking competent material when they're actually liquefiable under seismic loading. Our field crew adjusts geophone spacing and source energy based on target depth and site noise: a 2 m receiver interval with a 6 kg sledgehammer works for most 20–40 m profiles, but near Highway 1 or the rail corridor we switch to accelerated weight-drop sources to punch through ambient vibration. Processing runs through iterative inversion with ray coverage analysis; we flag cells with fewer than 15 ray hits so you know where resolution drops. On several Langley sites we’ve mapped paleochannels at 8–15 m depth that were completely missed by a 30 m grid of boreholes. When the tomography reveals soft zones near planned footing elevations, we recommend pairing results with in-situ permeability testing to evaluate drainage and consolidation timelines before finalizing foundation type.

Regional considerations

Langley sits at roughly 100 m elevation across much of the Township, sloping down toward the Fraser River floodplain on the north side. That gradient means site conditions change fast—glacial till on the uplands transitions to soft organic silts and peat in the lowlands within a few hundred metres. A seismic profile that only covers the building footprint can miss a deep paleochannel cutting across the corner of the lot, leaving a differential settlement problem nobody budgeted for. We’ve seen exactly that on several industrial projects near the Gloucester Industrial Park, where a 12 m deep buried channel showed up only because the tomography line extended beyond the pad corners. Liquefaction is another concern: NBCC 2020 places much of the Lower Mainland in a moderate-to-high seismic hazard zone, and velocity inversions (where softer material underlies stiffer crust) can hide liquefiable layers from refraction first-arrival picks. Full-waveform inversion or paired liquefaction assessment with CPT data closes that gap—and it’s far cheaper than discovering the problem during excavation.

Top questions

What depth can seismic tomography reach in Langley's glacial soils?

With a 115 m spread and a sledgehammer source, we typically resolve to 30–40 m depth in the compact glacial till common across upland Langley. In the softer alluvial areas near the Fraser floodplain, penetration drops to around 20–25 m due to higher attenuation. For deeper targets—say 60 m plus—we use an accelerated weight-drop or a small explosive charge with a longer spread. The inversion resolution also matters: we aim for cell sizes of 1–2 m vertically so thin layers aren’t smeared out.

How does seismic tomography help with NBCC 2020 site classification?

NBCC 2020 requires Vs30 (average shear-wave velocity in the top 30 m) to assign Site Class A through E. Seismic P-wave tomography gives us the layer geometry and P-wave velocities, but we need S-wave data for Vs30. We typically run a MASW survey along the same line or use crosshole S-wave measurements to get direct Vs profiles. The combination gives us both the velocity structure and the material boundaries, which is far more reliable than extrapolating from a single borehole log—especially in Langley where till thickness can vary 10 m across a single lot.

What does a seismic tomography survey cost for a typical Langley building lot?

For a standard commercial or light industrial lot in Langley with a 70–90 m refraction line and basic P-wave tomography, budgets generally fall between CA$4,110 and CA$6,660. The range depends on access conditions, source type (sledgehammer versus weight-drop), and whether we need to integrate MASW or crosshole data for Vs30. Densely treed sites, heavy traffic near the line, or night-work requirements push toward the upper end. We provide a fixed-price proposal after reviewing your site plan and target depths.

Can seismic tomography detect organics or peat layers?

Yes, and in Langley that’s one of its most useful applications. Peat and organic silts have dramatically lower P-wave velocities—often 300–600 m/s compared to 1,200–2,000 m/s for dense till. On a tomogram they show up as distinct low-velocity zones. The challenge is that saturated peat can sometimes look slightly faster than dry peat, so we always ground-truth with a test pit or borehole at one or two locations along the line. Once calibrated, the tomography maps the lateral extent of the organic layer far better than interpolating between point soundings.
