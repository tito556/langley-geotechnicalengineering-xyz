Too many site investigations in Langley stop at a handful of boreholes and leave half the property uncharacterized. When the Fraser Valley glacial sequence hides abandoned channels or perched water tables, that gamble gets expensive. Electrical resistivity fills the gap between discrete drill points, tracing stratigraphic boundaries and fluid pathways across the entire parcel before excavation begins. Our field crew deploys multi-electrode arrays and vertical electrical sounding profiles calibrated to local Quaternary geology—glacial till, advance outwash, and post-glacial silts that dominate the Langley basin. For projects where drill access is limited, we often pair resistivity lines with test pits to ground-truth anomalies at key locations, ensuring the geophysical model matches what the bucket actually encounters.

Resistivity data turns a handful of boreholes into a continuous subsurface model—essential in Langley’s variable glacial terrain.

Method and coverage

Langley sits on a complex stack of Vashon Drift, Capilano Sediments, and Fort Langley Formation deposits, creating resistivity contrasts that range from 10 ohm-m in saturated silts to over 500 ohm-m in clean sand and gravel bodies. The Township’s rolling uplands and low-lying floodplain produce variable water table depths—often within 2 to 5 m of surface on the Aldergrove aquifer margins. Vertical Electrical Sounding resolves these layered sequences efficiently, delivering apparent resistivity curves that invert to true layer thicknesses and resistivities. When the target is aggregate prospecting or mapping saline intrusion near the Nicomekl River, we switch to dipole-dipole or Wenner-Schlumberger 2D lines. Either way, the data integrates directly with CPT logs to build a continuous geotechnical cross-section without the blind spots that single-point methods leave behind.

Regional considerations

Langley’s growth from a Hudson’s Bay Company fort into a Township of 150,000 has pushed development onto terrain that older builders avoided—peat-filled depressions, buried creek valleys, and slopes mantled with colluvium. A resistivity survey that misses a low-resistivity clay lens beneath a proposed stormwater infiltration basin can trigger costly redesign when the system fails a percolation test. Similarly, high-resistivity sand lenses misinterpreted as dry can still carry perched water that floods excavations in November. The regulatory environment under the BC Water Sustainability Act and Township groundwater protection bylaws adds another layer: you need defensible baseline data before dewatering or discharging. Resistivity profiles, ground-truthed with targeted drilling, provide exactly that.

Complementary services

01

1D Vertical Electrical Sounding (VES)

Schlumberger array soundings for layer thickness and resistivity. Ideal for aggregate deposit delineation and groundwater table mapping in the Aldergrove and Brookswood areas.

02

2D Electrical Resistivity Tomography (ERT)

Multi-electrode profiles for lateral discontinuity detection. Used for cut-slope assessment, buried channel identification, and contaminant plume mapping along the Trans-Canada corridor.

03

Soil Corrosivity Assessment

Resistivity-based classification per CSA A23.3 for buried concrete and ductile iron pipe. Critical for infrastructure projects in the Township’s industrial zones.

Resistivity surveys tied to monitoring well data for delineating the Hopington and Aldergrove aquifers, supporting dewatering permit applications and long-term resource management.

Top questions

How much does an electrical resistivity survey cost in Langley?

Budget between CA$750 and CA$1,290 for a targeted 1D sounding or a short 2D line, depending on depth of investigation, electrode count, and site access conditions. Larger grid surveys or multi-line ERT programs are scoped project-by-project.

How deep can VES and ERT image in Langley’s soils?

1D VES routinely reaches 50 to 60 metres depth with sufficient electrode spread. 2D ERT typically resolves features to 25 or 30 metres, though depth of investigation depends on array geometry and the resistivity contrast of the target layer.

Can resistivity distinguish between glacial till and outwash sand in the Fraser Valley?

Yes. Till typically exhibits intermediate resistivity (30–60 ohm-m) while clean advance outwash sands exceed 150 ohm-m. The contrast is strong enough that VES curves reliably map the till-outwash interface across the Langley uplands.

Do I still need boreholes if I run a resistivity survey?

Resistivity reduces the number of boreholes needed but does not eliminate them. We recommend a minimum of two calibration points—boreholes or test pits—to constrain the inversion model and verify the lithology at resistivity boundaries.
